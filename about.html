The Origin

Both my parents were gone by the time I was seven.

My mother died in a car accident on Fair Day 1982. I was six years old, begging to go with her that night. She said no. Six weeks later, my father — a physician, a poet, a man who loved my mother so completely he couldn't survive without her — was gone too. I was seven.

"I sat eating cereal with my brother the morning after we lost Daddy, and I said: I don't know how to braid my own hair. I don't know how to cook toast. I have to learn to do all these things to take care of us. That was the moment survival mode began."

I was raised by my Papa — my father's brother — who left his career in Kuwait, moved his family to Texas, and became the most structurally loving man I have ever known. He was not easy on me. But he showed up every day. When I finally told him about the childhood sexual abuse I had carried in secret for five years, he didn't get angry at me. He started hitting his own head — grief that he couldn't protect me. That response healed something in me that nothing else could.

I grew up watching two men love me differently. My biological father, Daddy, measured love in distance — ultimately leaving. My father who raised me, Papa, measured it in presence — always there. By the time I was old enough to understand the difference, I had already learned to perform for love instead of receive it.

I became a national Bharatanatyam dance champion at 12. I got kicked out of college my freshman year. I became an ICU nurse at Northwestern Memorial Hospital. I fell in love with a cardiologist for seven years who would never say the words. I got married to my best friend, had a son, got divorced. Led clinical informatics across 78 hospitals. Got laid off. Lived on a houseboat. Survived a relationship that finally broke my nervous system open.

Throughout all of it, people would interact with me and see someone naturally uninhibited — fully alive, unafraid, walking into rooms like she owned the air in them. They would say: I'll have what she's having. That's actually where the book title comes from. But honestly? I wouldn't wish my life on anyone. What they were seeing wasn't the absence of pain. It was what I had built on top of it.

I had survived everything else by performing better, working harder, becoming more useful. But this time the system that had carried me through every hard thing — the drive, the competence, the show — stopped working. For the first time in my life, I couldn't perform my way out of it — not by working harder or doing more. In fact I realized I had to do the opposite and just sit with the painful feelings I had spent a lifetime performing around, achieving past, and refusing to feel. That was when I finally asked for help. That was the moment I stopped auditioning.

"In my therapist's office, I was reading an angry letter to a photo of my dead father — and I finally admitted I was furious at him for leaving. That was the beginning of everything."

In 2019, sitting across from my CBT counselor Russ Crites, I heard him say: "Anju, you are not a human doing. You are a human being." I argued with him. I said you have to prove your worth to be loved. He looked at me with pain in his eyes and said: that is not true. You have been spending a lifetime putting on a show.

That session became the seed of ICU-1111. The book. The frameworks. The work. I didn't write I'll Have What SHE's Having! to teach anyone anything. I wrote it to survive. And in writing it, I found the framework that changed everything — the DEAL Framework and the PAST Framework — built from the wreckage of a real life, not a theory.

People see my light and say: I'll have what she's having. They don't know I built it from the bottom. The healing wasn't recovery. It was artistry — taking the raw material of what happened and working it into something that has form, meaning, and eventually, beauty. That's what I help people do. Not fix what's broken. Build something with it.

Kicked Out of College Shame. Loss of reputation. Not good enough. My front tooth fell out. The degrees, the career, the frameworks — all of it came from a decision made at rock bottom to rise anyway.
Divorce Loss. Shame. Lost hair in clumps. Wore artificial hair until I decided to treat the actual problem — the feelings about myself — not just the symptom. I did the internal work of releasing the shame — the story that the marriage ending meant something was fundamentally wrong with me. When I stopped carrying that verdict, the loss of self stopped too. The body always registers what the soul has resolved.
Laid Off Loss of reputation. Lost more teeth. I chose to stop defining my worth by a title someone else could take away. ICU-1111, the book, the frameworks — none of that came from a job description. It came from deciding that my reputation would be built on what I created, not what I was given.
